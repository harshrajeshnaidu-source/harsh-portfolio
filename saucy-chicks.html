01 · Context

A food brand with no runway, no audience, and no margin for slow.

Saucy Chicks is a Canadian ghost kitchen concept — a chicken tender and sandwich brand built for delivery, living entirely across Uber Eats, DoorDash, and Skip the Dishes. No physical storefront. No walk-in traffic. No brand recognition. Just a menu and a mandate to build.

In the ghost kitchen model, the brand is the marketing. You don't have a location doing the heavy lifting. Every order comes through digital discovery — either through the platform algorithm, paid media, or organic content. If nobody knows you exist, nobody orders.

The task was as complete as it gets: build the brand identity, shoot the product photography, establish the content presence, get listed and optimised across three platforms, and drive initial order volume — all simultaneously, all within 60 days.

The constraint: Ghost kitchen brands have no location advantage, no word-of-mouth from foot traffic, and no physical brand expression. Every single touchpoint — platform listing, photo, social post, ad — has to do more work than it would for a brick-and-mortar.

Starting conditions
  • No brand assets — logo, colour system, or tone of voice
  • No product photography — every dish needed to be shot
  • No platform presence — zero reviews, zero history
  • No social accounts — starting from absolute zero followers
  • No paid media account or prior audience data to target

02 · Problem

Everything needed building. Nothing existed yet.

Most marketing briefs ask you to grow something that already exists. This one asked for something different: create the foundation and then grow it simultaneously. The brand had to look credible from day one — which meant the photography, the listing copy, the social feed, and the ad creative all had to be strong at launch, not after iteration.

The ghost kitchen category is brutally competitive on delivery platforms. Uber Eats and DoorDash surface restaurants by algorithm — a combination of ratings, completion rate, price, and proximity. For a new brand with zero reviews, breaking into that algorithm requires strong visual differentiation and an early order velocity push to signal relevance.

The problem was therefore threefold: build a brand that looked established before it was, drive enough early orders to activate the platform algorithm, and do it across three platforms at once without the content or budget to treat each one differently.

The three simultaneous challenges
Brand credibility on day one
No brand history to lean on. Photography and visual identity had to communicate quality instantly.
Platform algorithm activation
Zero reviews, zero history. Needed early order velocity to signal to the platforms that Saucy Chicks was worth surfacing.
Content at scale, lean budget
Three platforms, a social presence, and paid media — all with a startup-level content budget and a 60-day window.
03 · Strategy

Brand-first. Algorithm second. Paid third.

The sequencing mattered. In the ghost kitchen model, paid media without strong brand visuals and platform credibility wastes budget — you drive traffic to a listing that doesn't convert. I committed to building the brand foundation in weeks 1-2 before spending a dollar on paid, so that every channel worked harder from the moment it switched on.

Weeks 1–2
Brand identity and photography
Logo, colour system, brand voice, and full product photography shoot. Every menu item photographed for platform listings and social content.
Week 3
Platform setup and listing optimisation
Menu listings built across Uber Eats, DoorDash, and Skip with SEO-informed copy, photography uploaded, pricing strategy set.
Weeks 3–4
Social presence and content calendar launch
Instagram and TikTok launched. Initial content batch published. Tone and visual language locked in from day one.
Weeks 4–8
Paid media activation and optimisation
Meta campaigns launched targeting local food delivery audience. Platform-native promotions activated to drive early order velocity and first reviews.
Day 60
200,000+ content views
Organic and paid combined to hit 200K+ views. Platform listings active and generating orders across all three apps.

The key sequencing insight: In a brand-new DTC food business, creative quality is a performance variable, not a nice-to-have. Paid media directed at weak visuals wastes every dollar. We shot first, spent second — and the ROAS on paid reflected that discipline.

04 · Execution

What building a food brand actually looks like.

This case was unusual because the work was genuinely full-stack — brand, creative, photography, copy, platform ops, social, and paid all lived in the same sprint. Here's how each layer was built and why the sequencing held.

Layer 01
Brand identity system
Built the complete visual identity — logo, colour palette, typography, and brand voice. The positioning was deliberate: bold, irreverent, and unapologetically flavour-forward. The name "Saucy Chicks" set a tone that the entire visual system had to honour. Brand guidelines documented before a single post went live to ensure consistency across every channel from day one.
Brand Identity Visual System Positioning Brand Guidelines
Layer 02
Food photography and creative production
Directed the full product photography shoot — every menu item, every combo, hero shots and detail shots. Shot specifically for two use cases: platform listings (high-contrast, appetite-forward, optimised for small card format) and social/paid media (lifestyle framing, texture-focused, shareable). A single shoot produced the entire creative library used across all channels for the first 60 days.
Food Photography Creative Direction Content Production
Layer 03
Platform listing optimisation
Built all three platform listings from scratch — menu structure, item descriptions, pricing, photography upload, and promotion setup. Menu copy was written with search discoverability in mind: delivery platform algorithms surface restaurants based on item names and descriptions. Every description was written to be both conversion-optimised and keyword-rich for platform search.
Platform SEO Conversion Copy Menu Architecture Promotions Strategy
Layer 04
Social content system
Launched Instagram and TikTok simultaneously. Content batched from the photography shoot, supplemented with video content showing preparation, texture, and sauce coverage — the visuals that drive food sharing behaviour. Established a content cadence that kept the feed active without requiring a constant production effort. Organic social was treated as an awareness and credibility engine, not a conversion channel.

Layer 05
Paid media and early order velocity
Meta campaigns launched once the brand had visual credibility. Targeted locally — food delivery is a hyper-local channel, and geographic targeting kept CAC efficient. Platform-native promotions on Uber Eats and DoorDash drove first orders and seeded the initial review base, which then fed the organic algorithm. Paid retargeted social engagers who hadn't ordered yet.

06 · Learnings

What building from zero teaches you.

L01
Creative is a performance variable before it's an aesthetic one. In food delivery, the image on the listing card is the most important conversion asset in the entire funnel. Before the customer reads the description or the price, they've already judged the food. Getting the photography right before anything else was the single most important sequencing decision in this build.
L02
Platform algorithms reward early velocity — so you have to manufacture it. Ghost kitchen brands can't rely on organic discovery from foot traffic. The only way to get into the Uber Eats algorithm is to generate early orders, which requires active promotion. Platform discounts and promotions at launch aren't margin giveaways — they're algorithm investment.
L03
One well-planned shoot outperforms continuous content production. Batching 60 days of content from a single shoot is more efficient, more consistent, and higher quality than producing content piecemeal. The constraint of a single shoot forces better creative planning — you think harder about which shots you actually need, which produces a more purposeful content library.
L04
Brand consistency from day one compounds faster than retrofitting it later. Setting brand guidelines before the first post and enforcing them across every channel — listing copy, social captions, ad creative — meant the brand felt established immediately, even with zero history. Audiences don't know you're new unless your brand tells them you are.
