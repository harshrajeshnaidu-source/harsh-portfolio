01 · Context

A regulated investment platform where precision is non-negotiable.

Questrade is one of Canada's largest online investment brokerages, serving hundreds of thousands of retail investors with self-directed trading, managed portfolios, and foreign exchange services. It operates under IIROC regulatory oversight — one of the strictest compliance frameworks in Canadian financial services.

In this environment, a client issue isn't just an unhappy customer — it's a potential regulatory event. Every case involving account access, trade execution, fund movement, or reporting discrepancy carries real stakes: client financial risk, regulatory exposure, and reputational consequence if handled incorrectly or resolved too slowly.

My role sat at the intersection of client operations and internal process improvement. I handled complex investigations — not the first-tier "how do I log in" inquiries, but the cases that required tracing exactly what happened, why it happened, where in the system it broke, and how to prevent it recurring.

The environment in one sentence: In a regulated fintech, every client issue is simultaneously a service problem, a data signal, and a potential compliance event. Resolving it fast isn't enough — resolving it correctly, documenting it precisely, and learning from it systematically is the whole job.

Operating constraints
  • IIROC regulatory framework — documentation and accuracy mandatory
  • Issues involving real client funds and trading accounts
  • Zero tolerance for repeated systemic failures
  • Resolution accuracy more important than speed alone
  • Every case a potential pattern in a larger system problem
02 · Problem

Tickets were being closed. Patterns weren't being read.

Client service operations at scale face a structural trap: the volume of incoming issues creates pressure to resolve and close as quickly as possible. Efficiency becomes measured by throughput — cases closed per day, average handle time, queue depth.

The problem with that frame is that it treats every issue as independent. It doesn't ask whether ten different clients reporting slightly different symptoms are actually encountering the same underlying system failure. Individual tickets get closed. The root cause generating them stays open.

At Questrade, the issues coming in weren't random. Account access problems clustered after platform updates. Trade execution discrepancies appeared more frequently in specific instrument categories. Reporting errors tracked to particular account types. But without a framework for cross-case pattern detection, each case was being resolved individually — the same fix applied repeatedly, to new instances of the same problem.

The cost wasn't just operational inefficiency. In a regulated environment, recurring systemic issues that aren't identified and addressed carry increasing compliance risk with each repetition.

The gap in the existing approach
Tickets resolved, not analysed
Each case closed in isolation. No cross-case reading for common signals or root cause clustering.
Root causes not surfaced
Fixes applied to symptoms. Underlying system or process failures generating them went unaddressed.
No improvement loop
Insights from investigations weren't feeding back into process or system changes. The cycle repeated.
03 · Strategy

Treat every issue as a data point in a larger system.

The strategic reframe was precise: stop thinking of client issues as problems to resolve, and start treating them as signals to read. A single ticket tells you about one client's experience. A hundred tickets, pattern-matched correctly, tell you something is structurally wrong — and where.

I built a four-step investigation framework that changed how cases moved from intake to closure — adding a pattern-detection layer and a feedback loop that didn't exist before.

01
Issue received — intake, categorise, prioritise
Every incoming case logged with structured metadata: issue type, account category, product affected, channel of origin, and initial severity flag. Categorisation isn't just filing — it's the first layer of pattern data. Cases that look different on the surface often share a category signature that points to a common root.
Intake Categorisation Priority Triage
02
Investigate root cause — data, process, or system
Every case traced to its actual failure point — not the symptom the client reported, but the source of that symptom. Three possible origins: a data error (wrong information in a field or record), a process failure (something was done incorrectly or out of sequence), or a system failure (a platform or integration bug). Root cause determines resolution path and documentation requirements.
Root Cause Analysis Data Investigation Process Tracing
03
Detect patterns — cross-case signal recognition
The layer that didn't exist before. Before closing a case, it was cross-referenced against recent cases of the same category, product, and root cause type. Three or more cases sharing a root cause within a rolling window triggered a formal pattern flag — escalated for systemic review rather than individual resolution. This is where individual tickets became system intelligence.
Pattern Recognition Cross-case Analysis Signal Detection Escalation Framework
04
System improvement — insight to process fix, documented
Pattern flags triggered structured improvement cycles: identify the systemic failure, document the fix or process change required, implement with the relevant team, and close the loop with a documented record. This created a feedback mechanism where client issues directly drove operational improvement — not occasionally, but systematically, every time a pattern was detected.
Process Improvement Documentation Cross-functional Collaboration Feedback Loop

The key insight: The investigation framework didn't slow down case resolution — it added a parallel analysis layer. Individual cases were still resolved at the same pace. But the pattern-detection step meant that systemic issues were caught earlier, escalated correctly, and fixed upstream rather than resolved downstream over and over.

04 · Execution

What pattern-recognition looks like in practice.

The framework was applied across a range of issue categories that are common in retail investment platforms. Below is a representative cross-section of the signal types I worked with — illustrating how the same four-step framework produced different outcomes depending on whether the root cause was data, process, or system.

Reporting discrepancy Account balance display error Data Tracked to specific currency conversion calculation — integration bug

The regulated environment added a layer of precision to everything. In a standard client service role, a resolved case might mean the client is satisfied and the ticket is closed. At Questrade, resolution meant: the client's situation is corrected, the root cause is documented, the regulatory record is complete, and the fix is traceable if audited.

This standard made the pattern-detection work more impactful, not less. Because every case was already documented with precision, cross-case analysis could be done on clean, structured data — not informal notes. The compliance requirement that felt like overhead was actually the infrastructure that made systemic improvement possible.

Working cross-functionally was essential. When a pattern flag identified a system failure, the fix required coordination with product, engineering, or compliance teams — not just the operations function. Building credibility as someone who came to those conversations with precise root cause data, not just a complaint about volume, made those collaborations productive rather than territorial.

The improvement loop worked because it was grounded in evidence. "We're seeing 14 cases in the last 10 days with the same root cause in the currency conversion calculation" is a different conversation than "clients keep complaining about balance display." One is a data-backed escalation. The other is noise.

Context 01
Why regulated environments demand more precision
Every investigation record at Questrade was a potential audit document. Accuracy wasn't a quality standard — it was a regulatory requirement. This forced a level of rigour in root cause documentation that made the pattern data genuinely reliable.
Context 02
Why pattern detection requires volume to work
A single case is an anecdote. Three cases with the same root cause is a signal. Ten is a system failure. The framework only works because Questrade's scale provided enough case volume for patterns to emerge in statistically meaningful windows.
Context 03
Why the feedback loop was the most valuable part
Without the improvement loop, pattern detection would have been an interesting observation with no operational consequence. The loop — pattern detected, escalated, fixed, documented — is what turned client intelligence into actual system improvement.

06 · Learnings

What working in a regulated environment teaches you.

L01
Precision is a transferable skill. The level of documentation accuracy required in an IIROC-regulated environment is higher than almost any other professional context. Once you've worked to that standard — where every claim needs to be traceable, every resolution needs to be auditable — it changes how you approach data and evidence in every subsequent role. The discipline compounds.
L02
The most valuable thing you can do with a problem is ask whether it's actually one problem or a hundred problems with the same root. Most operational environments are optimised to close tickets fast. The slow, counterintuitive move is to pause before closing and ask: have I seen this before? Is something generating this systematically? That question — asked consistently — is where operations becomes strategy.
L03
Compliance requirements and good data practices aren't in tension — they're the same thing. The regulatory documentation standards at Questrade felt like overhead until I realised they were the infrastructure that made systemic analysis possible. Clean, structured, mandatory records are the foundation of any pattern-detection system. The constraint was the capability.
L04
Systems thinking applies everywhere — not just in marketing. The same mental model I used at Questrade — treat every data point as a signal in a larger system, look for patterns before drawing conclusions, build feedback loops that improve the system not just the symptom — is the exact frame I bring to growth work. The contexts are different. The thinking is identical.
