01 · Context

A fintech brand with no search presence.

LenDenClub is one of India's leading peer-to-peer lending platforms — connecting individual borrowers with retail investors at scale. The product was solid. The regulatory standing was strong. But the organic search presence was almost nonexistent.

Competitors were ranking for the keywords that mattered most — "personal loan online," "P2P investment India," "short-term loan instant approval" — while LenDenClub was invisible at the moment of intent. This wasn't a brand awareness problem. It was an acquisition infrastructure problem.

The opportunity was clear: fintech search queries carry some of the highest commercial intent of any vertical. Own the right keywords at the right moment, and you own the pipeline.

The core insight: SEO in fintech isn't about publishing more content. It's about mapping the exact language a borrower or investor uses at every stage of their decision, then building architectured landing pages that match that intent precisely.

Market context
  • High-intent search volume for P2P lending growing 40%+ YoY
  • Competitors ranked on 500+ keywords LenDenClub wasn't targeting
  • RBI-regulated fintech = trust signals critical in content
  • Dual audience: borrowers and investors, each with distinct intent
02 · Problem

Three compounding problems.

When I mapped the existing state, three distinct failure points emerged — each amplifying the others.

No keyword architecture. The site had content, but it was unfocused. Pages competed with each other. No clear topical authority had been established in any cluster. Google had no clear signal about what LenDenClub wanted to rank for.

No intent-matched landing pages. Traffic that did arrive hit generic pages. The conversion funnel was broken at the landing layer — high bounce rates, low time-on-page, and almost no form completion from organic visitors.

No paid-organic feedback loop. Paid and organic were running as siloed channels. Paid data wasn't informing which organic pages to build. Organic learnings weren't feeding paid targeting. The two channels were spending independently without compounding.

03 · Strategy

Architecture first. Content second.

The strategic frame was simple: treat SEO as infrastructure, not content production. Before writing a single article, I mapped the full keyword universe into intent clusters, identified the highest-value gaps, and designed a content architecture that would establish topical authority across both the borrower and investor journeys.

Phase 01
Keyword architecture and intent mapping
Built a full keyword universe across both the borrower journey (personal loan, instant credit, P2P borrow) and investor journey (P2P investment, high returns, alternative assets). Clustered by intent stage — informational, navigational, transactional. Mapped competitors' ranking positions to identify gap clusters with high volume and low difficulty.

Phase 02
Content system and topical authority build
Designed a hub-and-spoke content model. Pillar pages targeting primary intent clusters, supported by satellite articles targeting long-tail variations. Each piece was written with conversion in mind — trust signals, regulatory credentials, and clear CTAs baked into every template. Established editorial standards and a production cadence that could scale.

Phase 03
Intent-matched landing page system
Built a library of landing pages matched to specific keyword intents — not generic product pages, but pages designed around the exact query a user typed. Each page had a dedicated value proposition, social proof set, and CTA calibrated to the intent stage. Borrower and investor pages were built as entirely separate conversion flows.

Phase 04
Paid amplification and feedback loop
Used paid search and Meta to amplify the highest-performing organic pages and to test new keyword clusters before committing organic resource. Paid campaigns retargeted organic visitors who hadn't converted, and paid data directly informed which new organic content to build next. The two channels ran as a unified system, not separate budgets.

04 · Execution

The acquisition loop in practice.

Execution was built around a single principle: every channel feeds the next. SEO drives intent-qualified traffic. Landing pages capture it. Paid retargets what didn't convert. Email nurtures what's in pipeline. Each loop makes the next iteration smarter.

The acquisition loop

SEO layer: Over 12 months, the content system produced 250+ pages ranking in the top 3 positions for high-intent fintech queries. The architecture was built so that new pages benefited from the domain authority of existing clusters — each piece of content compounded the previous.

Paid layer: Google Search ads ran on the same intent clusters as organic, capturing demand that SEO hadn't yet ranked for. Meta campaigns retargeted organic visitors and lookalike audiences based on the highest-converting lead profiles. Paid budget was reallocated weekly based on organic performance data.

Email layer: A segmented nurture sequence handled the gap between lead capture and conversion — separate tracks for borrowers and investors, with different objection handling and trust signal sequencing. Open rates averaged 38%+, driving consistent pipeline contribution.

The data loop: Every channel reported into a unified dashboard. Paid CTR data informed which organic titles to test. Organic keyword data informed which paid ad copy to write. Conversion data from landing pages fed back into content briefs. Nothing ran in isolation.

06 · Learnings

What this reinforced.

L01
SEO is architecture, not content volume. The brands that win in search don't publish more — they build more intentionally. Topical authority is earned through structured coverage of an intent universe, not random article production. Every page needs a reason to exist in the architecture.
L02
Paid and organic are most powerful when they're talking to each other. The moment I unified the data view across both channels, performance improved on both sides. Paid found better audiences. Organic found better angles. The silos were costing money on both sides.
L03
In regulated verticals, trust is a conversion variable. Fintech content that doesn't explicitly address regulatory standing, security, and risk performs significantly worse than content that does. Compliance signals aren't just legal requirements — they're persuasion tools. Writing them that way changes everything.
L04
Compound returns take time but are worth waiting for. The system didn't reach full velocity until month 8. Months 1-4 were mostly invisible. Any stakeholder expecting quick organic wins would have pulled the budget too early. The job of the strategist is partly to protect the long game from short-term pressure.
