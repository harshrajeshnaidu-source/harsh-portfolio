01 · Context

Five brands. One ops layer. Constant pressure.

Tata Digital is the digital arm of the Tata Group — one of India's most iconic conglomerates. Its mandate: build and operate a portfolio of consumer digital products across verticals, anchored by the Tata Neu super app.

The brands I worked across spanned fintech, travel, retail, wellness, and hospitality. Each had its own brand identity, tone of voice, target audience, campaign calendar, and performance KPIs. Each demanded creatives that looked and felt brand-native — not templated, not generic.

The challenge wasn't any single campaign. It was running all of them simultaneously without the quality, timeline, or brand standards of any one slipping. At enterprise scale, operational excellence is a competitive advantage in its own right.

The environment: Enterprise marketing ops at this scale isn't just project management. It's systems thinking applied to people, process, and creative production simultaneously — while the campaigns are live and deadlines don't move.

Operating environment

  • Multiple agencies, studios, and internal teams in production pipeline
  • Creatives required across every major paid channel and format
  • Brand standards non-negotiable — each brand had distinct visual identity

02 · Problem

Volume, velocity, and brand integrity — simultaneously.

The core tension at Tata Digital wasn't a lack of talent or budget — it was a structural one. The organisation had scaled its brand portfolio faster than its production infrastructure. The result: a creative ops function that was permanently reactive, always catching up, always at risk of a brand inconsistency slipping through at volume.

Volume problem: 25,000+ creatives across 5 brands is an extraordinary throughput requirement. At that scale, any manual review step, any unclear brief template, or any approval bottleneck multiplies into delays across the entire portfolio.

Brand integrity problem: With multiple agencies and internal teams producing simultaneously, creative consistency was difficult to enforce. Each brand had its own visual language — and at enterprise scale, one off-brand creative in a live campaign is a reputational risk, not just a design error.

The three failure modes to solve
Production bottlenecks
Brief-to-delivery pipelines that slowed under volume, missing launch windows.
Brand consistency at scale
Off-brand creative making it to live without a scalable QA layer.
Cross-brand coordination
Five brands competing for the same internal resources, with no priority framework.
03 · Strategy

Build the machine before running it faster.

The instinct under pressure is to add more people or more hours. The right move at this scale is to fix the process — so that every additional creative produced flows through a system that enforces quality automatically rather than relying on individual heroics. I focused on three structural changes before touching any individual campaign.

Phase 01
Standardised brief architecture
Built a brief template system for each brand — structured briefs that captured audience, intent, format specs, tone guardrails, and performance objective in a single document. Reduced brief-to-brief-approval cycle from days to hours. Eliminated the most common source of revision cycles: ambiguity at briefing stage.
Brief Templates Process Design Stakeholder Alignment
Phase 02
Creative QA framework
Designed a brand-by-brand QA checklist that any reviewer could run — not just senior creatives. Covered visual identity, tone, legal/compliance flags, format specifications, and platform requirements per channel. Moved QA from a judgement call to a structured review that scaled across team members and agencies without losing brand standards.
Quality Assurance Brand Governance Production Standards
Phase 03
Cross-brand prioritisation model
Built a resource allocation framework that ranked competing brand demands against strategic priority, revenue impact, and campaign readiness. Gave the ops function a defensible way to sequence work and push back on ad hoc requests — without creating brand politics or blocking urgent launches.
Resource Planning Stakeholder Management Priority Framework
Phase 04
Campaign performance tracking and iteration loop
Consolidated campaign performance reporting across brands into a single weekly view — click-through rates, conversion rates, ROAS, and creative fatigue signals by brand and format. Used this data to inform which creative formats to double down on, which to retire, and where to test new angles. Performance insight fed directly back into the next brief cycle.

04 · Execution

What 25,000 creatives actually looks like.

At enterprise scale, execution isn't about doing things harder — it's about doing things in the right order, through the right structure. Here's what the production system looked like in practice across the 5-brand portfolio.

Production pipeline: Each campaign moved through a defined flow — brief → agency/studio briefing → draft review → QA checklist → approval → trafficking → live monitoring. Every step had an owner and a timeframe. Nothing waited in an inbox.

Tata Neu as anchor: As the super app that connected all five brands, Tata Neu campaigns required additional coordination — any Tata Neu creative that referenced another brand had to be approved by both brand teams. I managed this cross-brand approval layer without letting it become a bottleneck.

Creative iteration system: Instead of treating each campaign as a standalone production, I built a rolling creative library per brand — top-performing formats, copy angles, and visual treatments that could be adapted for new campaigns rather than rebuilt from zero each time. This reduced production time significantly on repeat campaign types.

Live monitoring: Once campaigns went live, daily performance monitoring across all brands flagged creative fatigue early — allowing underperforming creatives to be swapped before they dragged campaign ROAS, without waiting for a weekly review cycle.

06 · Learnings

What enterprise scale teaches you.

L01
Operational clarity is a creative advantage. When briefs are ambiguous, creatives compensate with their own interpretation. Sometimes that's inspired. At scale, it's a brand consistency risk. Structured briefs don't constrain creativity — they direct it toward the right problem. The best creative teams I've worked with are hungrier for a clear brief than a loose one.
L02
Systemising QA is not a creative compromise — it's a brand protection mechanism. Manual QA that relies on a senior person's eye doesn't scale. Building a checklist that any team member can run — and that catches brand, legal, and platform issues at the same time — protects the brand without adding senior time to every creative.
L03
Creative fatigue is a data problem, not a creative problem. Most brands replace underperforming creatives based on gut feel or when performance becomes obviously bad. Building an early-warning system around engagement rate decay and frequency meant we could act weeks before ROAS dropped — keeping the portfolio consistently fresh.
L04
At enterprise scale, saying no to unplanned work is a delivery skill. The single biggest threat to a multi-brand ops function is ad hoc requests that bypass the process. Building a prioritisation framework gave the team a principled, defensible way to sequence demands — and protected every planned campaign from being disrupted by the loudest voice in the room.
